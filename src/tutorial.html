Assembling a module to match our standards while reaching the best levels of detail possible can be tricky so we've gone ahead and done the following:

We have created a detailed tutorial to help guide you through the process and ensure your module is up to spec and for the best price and quality possible too!

This page is divided into chapters with detailed instructions for each step of the process.

ASSEMBLY

How to build a Light Rail Module

The first step to assembly is to press fit the laser cut components together. Ensure all parts are aligned properly before glueing them together.

It's recommended to use a small amount of wood glue and if possible try to clamp the parts together, aiming to have perfect 90-degree and/or 45-degree angles.

Always inspect the module for any misalignments or gaps and make adjustments if necessary.

Once a module is dry, you can move onto laying track.

Module Parts

Generally consisting of 4 sides + a top + pavement + sidewalk

Basic Assembly

Bare wood parts put together

Atlas Code 100 Flex Track is recommended for these modules. Available in 36" lengths, it's recommended to cut out a piece that overhangs the module's edge by about 1/2" to allow for fine triming further along.

PVA glue is recommended for securing the track to the module base. You'll want this as thin as possible to prevent the track from sitting too high.

We recommend puting some amount of weight ontop of the track while it cures to ensure proper adhesion and height.

Once fully dried, you can trim any excess track and proceed to wiring. We recommend using a dremel tool for precise trimming.

Track should be 100% flush with the module end - of course the expansion and contraction of materials should be noted and may require adjustments down the road.

Wiring

How to wire a Light Rail Module

The first step to wiring is placing your terminal blocks on the underside. You'll want to drill and screw these in, of course try to align them in such a way that doesn't end with a screw poking up into your track or street.

Once in place, feeder wire holes should be drilled through on the outer edges of the track in whatever location best suites one's needs.

22 AWG feeder wires will run down from the track to the 14 AWG bus wires.

Crimping feeder wires together with the bus wire into the fork terminals is the best approach. Make sure wires are in well and can survice a tug-test.

One can then easily screw them into the terminal blocks. This allows easy removal and replacement of wires if needed. Also is handy for troubleshooting.

Basic Module Wiring

Red - DCC +, Black - DCC -, Yellow - ACC +, Blue - ACC -

Station Module Wiring for Block Detection

White - BD20 +, Purple - BD20 -, Green - Station Detection, Gray - Station Detection

Modules are connected with colored Anderson Power Pole connectors. It's crucial to match the colors to prevent electrical issues and ensure proper operation.

Anderson Power Poles are crimped to the 14 AWG bus wires on one end and fork terminals on the other. Easy to replace if needed.

Station Modules have an additional two Purple connectors and a White connector for block detection.

They also have a Green and Grey connector to check for block detection on the second station module in a pair.

Block detection simply tells the system when a train is in a specific section of track, ie: a station, allowing for proper train control and signaling.

NCE BD20 Block Detectors are used and wired as seen in the graphic.

As seen in the graphics, the upper most rail is connected to the red, green, and grey feeder wires.

Somewhere inbetween on each, a cut needs to be made in the rail to isolate the section of track next to the station platform. This allows the block detector to properly detect when a train is in the station and control train movement accordingly.

Station Module Wiring for Block Detection

Red - DCC +, Black - DCC -, Yellow - ACC +, Blue - ACC -, Green - Station Detection, Gray - Station Detection

Master Control Station Module

Thick Black - NCE RJ12, Thick Dark Gray - Typical Power Wires, Thick Light Gray - Ethernet Cables

This particular panel in the graphic to the left is the 'Master Control' Module, which includes an NCE SB5 Smart Booster, a Power Supply, NCE to USB interface (which allows automation control using JMRI software), NCE AIU01 Auxiliary Input Unit, and typical of station modules, the NCE BD20 Block Detector.

This is only neccesary for complete automation of a layout, and only one is needed per layout too. A much simpler approach is fine for most users, such as a typical NCE PowerCab wired up directly to the bus.

PowerCON and USB panel mounts are used to make it easier to connect and disconnect power and USB cables from the modules.

Of course, it's up to the individual module owner to put DCC panels in there modules, however, we highly recommend using NCE UTP Cat5 Panels to allow connection with other modules with ethernet cables. These are often more affordable than standard NCE cables.

Once track is laid and wires are connected, you can move onto testing your wiring. Any technique works (ie: using a multimeter, running a DC or DCC locomotive and system).

Paving

How to pave a Light Rail Module

Paved Module

After the module is fully paved, it is ready for painting.

Paving over the rails, on top of the street, and the sidewalk is the next step.

This is done by using a mixture of sand, PVA glue, spackle, and a touch of whatever color acrylic paint you desire the pavement to look like.

Mix well, then apply a fairly thick enough layer over the track to completely fill it in on all sides. The tops of the rails should be showing. Spread the excess or apply a thin layer over the rest of the road surface too. We recommend even doing it to the sidewalk to give it some texture, however other techniques are okay too.

Before it all dries, run a knife or a spare set of HO scale trucks (rail car wheels) into the inner groove to create the gap necessary for trains to still run.

Painting

How to paint a Light Rail Module

Once fully dried, one can mask off and paint the sidewalk Apple Barrel Matte Pewter Gray. Cracking paint and tar patches can be painted onto the asphalt. Lane markers, crosswalks, and other features can be painted in. Followed up by dark grey/brown/black washes to add some contrast.

Station and catenary components can then be painted and installed. Other signage can be added as desired.

We recommend running LEDs off of Arduino's (and comparable products) which allows customization of the lighting features. Reference the accessory wiring for more information on that. Once all is assembled and functional, you have a completed module that’s ready for use!

Arduino

How to wire and program an Arduino for your Light Rail Module

LED's can be wired to an Arduino's outputs, then programmed to control what they do. This is useful if one wants to customize the lighting features of their module.

Station lights for example could be set to a steady on, but a nearby car could have a blinking turn signal running. Flashing emergency lights for various vehicles are another possibility, alongside functional stop lights.

Sound can be added too, such as crosswalk beeping, among other ambient sounds.

Moving components can be ran off servos and other motors, such as crossing gates, or turnout control for the switches in the track.

Wiring in an Arduino

Wiring the Arduino to the module's Accessory Bus

Wiring LED's and Other Features

Wiring up things to the arduino

Power for these run off the 12V 1A power supply that is part of the module's accessory bus. One will simply use a barrel connetor that branches off of that and plug it into the Arduino's power input.

The Arduino can programmed from the USB, but later ran off of the accessory bus power.

Any type of Arduino (or like product) can be used, so long as it can be powered by the 12V supply and has enough digital and analog pins to control the desired features.
